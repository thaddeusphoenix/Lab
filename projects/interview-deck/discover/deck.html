Project Scheduling
Relaunch at Sitetracker

From scheduling tech debt to production-ready enterprise infrastructure — a two-year build across three chapters.
Nathan Stankowski  ·  Lead PM
Context

B2B enterprise software for the infrastructure that powers the energy transition.

Solar, BESS, Wind, EV Charging — from "should we build?" through construction and commissioning to operations and asset management. One system of record for the whole lifecycle.

The Moment I Joined

The Company
2023. New PM leader, new PM team, new product strategy. The org had just rebuilt the PM function twice. The shift: from analysis paralysis and product silos to founder-mode, product-led growth. I was 12 months in when this story starts.
The Mission
Win market share in Solar Development and Construction. We had EV Charging references that opened the door. Now we needed sales that led with Solar use cases — and customers who could prove it. My role: start by defining and delivering the features. End by enabling the entire organization.
Chapter 1  ·  2024

Scheduling Tech Debt

Before writing a single spec, I needed to understand what we were actually working with.

Where the Dead Bodies Were Hidden

  • My lead engineer took me through the architecture. Three structural problems: a deep data lock between project and activity, a UI so fragile that the smallest change caused support pain, and scheduling logic tied to sync with massive compute overhead.
  • I went to Sales and Marketing next. Templates were being pitched as a strength — but Project Managers experienced them as a constraint. The central nervous system of schedule and process control was missing entirely.
  • This audit became the foundation for the entire 2-year strategy. I needed to know what was real before I could commit to anything.

Phase 1: Rebuild for Flexibility

  • I scoped Phase 1 around one goal: break the rigidity. Rebuild the UI so it could flex. Ship Ad Hoc Activities, Cross-Project Dependencies, and train the market on what the existing product could already do.
  • I ran Marketing and training campaigns on capabilities customers didn't know they had — not to obscure the gaps, but to build credibility while we closed them.
  • It worked. Sales increased in magnitude and velocity. Which is when things got complicated.
Chapter 2  ·  2025

Catastrophic Success

Three customers using the product successfully. Fifteen scheduled to go live in January. That's when the RED ALERT hit.
January 2025  ·  Red Alert

15 Customers.
Not Ready.

Three customers had gotten along with the 2024 improvements. Fifteen were going live in January.

The lesson: three is not enough signal. A successful sale does not equal product value realized or features understood. We had sold something we hadn't yet fully built.

The Hard Pivot

What we stopped
  • AI features — deprioritized entirely
  • Risk management roadmap — paused
  • Secondary feature expansion
  • Broad go-live schedule
What I did instead
  • 100% focus on scheduling overhaul — 6 to 12 months
  • Embedded PMs with every implementation team
  • Sharpened the roadmap to the critical path only
  • Declared implementation a product problem — my team owned it

Implementation Is a Product Problem

  • I stopped treating implementation as someone else's problem and took direct responsibility. My team would implement our beta partners, run production support, and own the launch outcome.
  • This wasn't a process change — it was a reframe. Time-to-Value is part of the product. If customers couldn't get live, the features didn't matter.
  • I launched a major internal PR campaign to realign the organization. Then I went to work on the three customers who could actually validate what we were building.
Chapter 3  ·  2026

Real Success

Three of the fifteen became critical beta partners. Deep embedding. Full production support as the guarantee. Monthly, testable, deployable deliverables.

Scrappy, Structured, Deliberate

  • I selected three beta partners and embedded my team directly in their implementations — full production support as the guarantee that we would not abandon them mid-launch.
  • Monthly deployable deliverables: Drag and Drop, Logic as Excel Data, UI for net-new projects, Migration tooling. Each one tested with real users before the next was scoped.
  • I ran retrospectives after every cycle. What broke, what held, what the customer actually needed vs. what we assumed. The roadmap updated every month.

AI — Back on the Roadmap, Differently

  • When AI came back into scope, it was grounded in core product value — not a feature layer on an unstable foundation.
  • I used AI to generate high-fidelity test scenarios — schedules for large Solar Plants and BESS systems — to pressure-test the CPM engine against real-world complexity before any customer saw it.
  • The north star: Implementation Zero — zero time-to-value for project scheduling. The AI roadmap is built around that goal: schedule coordination, migration, and dependency suggestion.

The Hardest Conversation

  • Sales was selling Advanced scheduling capabilities. Implementation was deploying Standard logic. Customers were getting something different from what they were promised.
  • I ran a workshop to align Sales on the two distinct frameworks. Then I restructured the implementation process: no deployment without sign-off from the customer's own Project Managers on the scheduling logic.
  • The pressure to make exceptions was constant — every account felt like a special case. I held the line. No exceptions. The process only works if it's universal.

What I'd Do Differently

  • Three is not enough signal. I'd instrument every stage — market interest, sales value, implementation progress, user adoption — from day one. Qualitative and quantitative, always. If it's not tracked, it's not managed.
  • GTM is part of the product. The Time-to-Value journey needs to be fully mapped and directly experienced by the product team through pilot implementations before you hand it to the rest of the org.
  • Earlier enablement. The shift from milestone tracking to CPM-based scheduling required a cultural change internally. I should have built CPM fundamentals into training from the start — not after the RED ALERT.
I prioritized a stable foundation over feature velocity — and when the catastrophic success moment hit, I treated the implementation crisis as a product problem instead of passing it to another team.

That decision is what turned fifteen potential failures into three real, referenceable customers.
