Sitetracker Project
Scheduling Relaunch

A 2-year strategic relaunch — from legacy tracking tool to modern system of record for critical infrastructure.
Nathan Stankowski  ·  Lead PM
01 — Context

Critical infrastructure PM software at enterprise scale.

Solar, BESS, Wind, EV Charging — the teams building this infrastructure run on project schedules. Sitetracker was their system of record.

The Setup

The Space
Critical infrastructure deployment — Solar, BESS, Wind, EV Charging. Enterprise customers running large capital projects with strict milestone and dependency requirements.
My Role
Lead PM for end-to-end product strategy. Responsible for transitioning the product from a legacy milestone tracker to a full scheduling system of record. Partnered with a Sr. Director of Engineering and a Staff SDET across a distributed US and India team.
02 — Inception

The Denver Summit: grounding strategy in technical reality.

Before writing a single spec, I needed to understand what we were actually working with.

The Denver Summit

  • Upon joining, I organized and led a 3-day technical summit in Denver with three senior engineers — two 10-year veterans and one new hire.
  • I led a session to document every piece of technical debt, architectural limitation, and failed feature request from previous years — so the 2-year strategy was grounded in technical reality, not wishful thinking.
  • The summit produced a clear picture of what the system could and couldn't support, and became the foundation for every prioritization decision that followed.

Two Scheduling Frameworks

Standard Scheduling
A templatized, milestone-centric approach for existing customers requiring simple tracking. Protected the base, preserved relationships.
Advanced Scheduling
A new activity-centric framework for enterprise customers requiring full CPM logic and high granularity. A clear opt-in path for new and growing accounts — without disrupting what already worked.
03 — AI as a Force Multiplier

Using AI to stress-test the engine before customers did.

Two distinct applications: validation before release, and transition tooling for customers migrating from legacy systems.

AI-Driven QA Strategy

  • I used AI to generate dozens of high-fidelity test scenarios — schedules for massive Solar Plants, BESS systems, and Industrial Solar — to pressure-test the CPM calculation engine against real-world complexity for the first time.
  • This gave the QA team realistic synthetic datasets to run performance and logic tests against, identifying and resolving CPM engine errors before release rather than in production.
  • The result: a significantly lower post-release bug rate and an engine the team had actual confidence in.

AI-Driven Schedule Import

  • To bridge the gap between frameworks, I led the development of an AI-driven schedule importing feature.
  • I designed the tool to ingest legacy formats and use AI to automatically map data and suggest dependencies — effectively upgrading a customer's old milestones into the new activity-based framework.
  • This reduced customer transition time by 90% and removed the primary adoption barrier for accounts moving off legacy tools like Primavera P6.
04 — Tradeoffs & Pivots

A whale account, an architectural principle, and a deliberate slowdown.

The hardest calls weren't about features — they were about what to protect when the pressure came.

The Key Calls

  • The pivot: A major enterprise account required complex cross-project dependencies earlier than planned. I pivoted engineering to address these to secure the contract.
  • The guardrail: I ensured those "whale" features were developed as modular extensions of the Advanced framework — not one-off codebases that would create future debt.
  • The deliberate slowdown: After the detour, I slowed secondary feature launches to address remaining architectural debt from Denver. A stable math engine was non-negotiable for long-term revenue growth.
05 — Handling Conflict

Sales was selling Advanced. Customers were getting Standard.

The gap between what was pitched and what was deployed was eroding trust — and it required a structural fix, not a conversation.

The Conflict & Resolution

  • I identified that Sales was marketing Advanced capabilities using Standard templates, and Implementation teams were deploying without involving the customer's actual Project Managers.
  • Customers were receiving Advanced pitches but being implemented on Standard logic — a gap between expectations and reality that would compound over time.
  • I facilitated a workshop to align Sales on the two distinct frameworks, and restructured the implementation process to require sign-off from the customer's PMs on scheduling logic before deployment.
  • I prioritized functional logic over marketing-led requests. The product had to deliver actual value to the end user — or the revenue wouldn't hold.

07 — What I'd Do Differently

GTM is part of the product. I learned this the hard way.

Lessons

  • GTM as product: The Time-to-Value journey must be fully mapped and directly experienced by the product team through pilot implementations before enabling the rest of the organization.
  • Reportable tracking: Every stage needs clear metrics — market interest, sales value, customer implementation, user adoption. If it's not tracked, it's not managed.
  • Earlier enablement: The cultural shift from Standard tracking to Advanced scheduling required more internal training. I should have built CPM fundamentals into the enablement process much earlier.
My approach was to prioritize a stable data model and use AI to validate that model under real-world stress.

That foundation allowed the product to scale across the enterprise and double revenue — while giving customers a credible path off legacy tools.
